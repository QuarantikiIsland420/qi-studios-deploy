01

The Premise

On The Brink Handyman Services is a Colorado-based home repair, maintenance, and IT solutions company. Most handyman businesses advertise with a phone number and a list of services. The Brinkman does something different: it makes the business a character.

The Brinkman is not a mascot. He's the company made human. He's the person you'd trust with a key to your house — the kind of guy who shows up on time, knows what he's looking at, fixes it right, and doesn't make it weird.

Every piece of content — every post, every caption, every video — comes from the character. Not a brand voice. Not a persona. A person with a Colorado drawl, a dry sense of humor, and thirty years of knowing how to fix things.

"He's the guy who shows up with the right tool, the right answer, and a coffee already in hand."
The Brinkman — Brand Premise
02

Who He Is

The Brinkman is a Colorado man in his 40s. He's not defined by an age — he's defined by what he knows and how he carries it. He's been doing this work long enough to have seen everything and been surprised by very little. His expertise isn't performed — it's just present.

He's the neighbor you always wanted. The one who knew what was wrong before you finished describing it. He doesn't need you to explain the problem. He just needs to see it.

He drives a truck that's seen some miles. He keeps his tools in order. He drinks his coffee black. He doesn't say much he doesn't mean.

Character Traits
  • Reliable without making it a thing
  • Skilled without being arrogant
  • Dry wit, never performative
  • Colorado-rooted, mountain-shaped
  • Quietly proud of doing things right
  • Low tolerance for overcomplicated
  • Loyal to clients who appreciate the work
What Drives Him
  • The satisfaction of a problem solved
  • Clients who don't second-guess the work
  • Colorado seasons, Colorado light
  • A job that's harder than it looks
  • Coffee, in that order
  • The truck running right
  • Knowing the estimate was fair

Visual Description

The Brinkman's look is specific. It is not a costume — it is a consistent visual signature that identifies the character across every piece of content, regardless of the medium. These parameters do not change. The situation changes. The setting changes. The character stays fixed.

Always Present
  • Red plaid flannel (the hero garment)
  • Work overalls or Carhartt-adjacent
  • Well-worn, serious work boots
  • A tool — wrench, drill, or at minimum a belt
  • A coffee cup (nearly always)
  • Colorado in the background — mountains, sky, or property
Character Color Palette
  • Red — the plaid, the hero accent
  • Off-white — the work shirt underneath
  • Weathered denim — the overalls
  • Warm amber — the light, the coffee
  • Mountain sage — the distance
  • Steel — the tools, the truck
"Imagery that looks like it was shot at 6am before a job — not in a studio."
Visual Direction Principle
Expression & Posture
  • Steady — not excited, not grim
  • Slightly amused by the situation
  • Arms crossed or hands occupied
  • Never posed, never performing
  • Looking at the problem, not the camera
What He Never Looks Like
  • Stock photo happy
  • Gym-built or polished
  • Studio lighting, white background
  • Uniform with a logo shirt (too corporate)
  • Surprised or flustered
04

Voice & Tone

The Brinkman's voice is the most important asset in this brand. Everything else can be adjusted. The voice cannot. It is specific, it is consistent, and it is built on understatement.

He doesn't oversell. He doesn't undersell. He says the true thing in as few words as it takes. The dry wit is not a style choice — it is a byproduct of someone who has seen every variation of a problem and has an efficient response to all of them.

When the job is obvious
"Yep. You should probably get that fixed."
When the job is done
"That'll hold."
When the client waited too long
"Probably should've called me sooner. But we're here now."
When the work speaks for itself
"Two hours. Parts included. Problem's gone."
On a complicated job
"I've seen worse. Not much, but some."
On why he does this
"You've got good bones. Just needs some attention."
Signing off
"That's it. Drive safe."
On expertise
"Yep. That's why you call."
He Says
  • Direct observations, plainly stated
  • Understatement over emphasis
  • Colorado-specific references — altitude, weather, mountain towns
  • Short sentences that end clean
  • Dry humor with no setup
  • Professional confidence with no arrogance
He Never Says
  • Marketing language or corporate speak
  • Exclamation points (almost ever)
  • Slang that ages ("slay," "no cap," "fire")
  • Self-deprecation about his skills
  • "Reach out" or "connect with us"
  • Anything that sounds like an ad
05

Content Pillars

All content produced for The Brinkman lives within four pillars. Every post, every video, every caption maps to one of these categories. The ratio should be roughly: 35% The Job, 30% The Character, 25% The Service, 10% Colorado.

01
The Job
Situation plus fix. Before and after. The problem, the assessment, and the result. Ends with "That'll hold." or something like it. This is the most shareable content — people tag their neighbors.
02
The Character
The Brinkman in the world. Memes. Quotes. The character in situation with no service to sell. This builds the relationship. People follow the character before they need the handyman.
03
The Service
Direct, clean service callouts. What The Brinkman does, what it costs, how to book. The Brinkman voice throughout — never generic. Never a laundry list.
04
Colorado
Seasonal content. Local references. The landscape The Brinkman lives and works in. This content earns the Colorado audience — it speaks to people who know the altitude, the winters, the specific problems that come from where they live.
06

Platform Playbook

The Brinkman's voice stays consistent across platforms. His register adjusts slightly — not the character, just the formality. Think of it as the same man in different rooms.

Facebook
"Direct. Local. Slightly more words."
  • Complete sentences. Short paragraphs.
  • Talk about specific services with a local angle
  • Photos of work — real jobs, not staged
  • End with a soft CTA, never pushy
  • Respond to comments in character
Instagram
"Visual first. Caption is the punchline."
  • Image carries the weight — The Brinkman in situation
  • Caption: 1–3 lines max. Often just one sentence.
  • Meme format works well here
  • No hashtag dump — 3–5 targeted max
  • Reels: character moment, no voiceover needed
Nextdoor / Angi
"Neighbor, not salesperson."
  • First-person but still in voice
  • Local specificity (neighborhood, area, season)
  • Lead with the service, not the character
  • Licensed and insured — mention it
  • One CTA: call or text the number
Google Business
"Professional. Warm. Brief."
  • Response to reviews: thank them, stay in voice
  • Posts: service updates, seasonal reminders
  • Photos: real work, Colorado backdrop
  • Description: The Brinkman, plain and useful
  • Reply to every review — it builds the algorithm

Fence post snapped clean off. Colorado winter'll do that to old wood.

Came out to take a look.

Post was rotten at the base — had been for a while. Replaced it, re-secured the rail, treated the wood.

Two hours. Problem's gone before the next storm.

That'll hold.

On The Brink — 720-555-0192

Situation · Reaction · Punchline
[THE SITUATION. Set the scene briefly — where The Brinkman is, what he's looking at.] [THE REACTION. What he says or thinks. Keep it dry.] [THE PUNCHLINE or observation. One sentence. Clean stop.]
