03 — Photography & Video

What It Looks Like

Every image in The Brinkman's world follows the same visual grammar. It does not look like marketing. It looks like documentation of a real person doing real work in a real place. The difference is intentional — it builds trust in a category where trust is everything.

Shoot This
  • 6am light — golden, directional, long shadows
  • Colorado landscape in frame — mountains, rural roads, real properties
  • The Brinkman at work or just after — tools in use
  • Coffee cup present or nearby
  • Truck in background (makes it real)
  • Before/after of the job — the problem and the fix
  • Warm color grade — amber push, slight desaturation
  • Cinematic framing — wide when the landscape matters
Never This
  • White studio backgrounds
  • Stock photo aesthetic — posed group smiles
  • Midday flat light — no shadows, no warmth
  • Generic suburbia — no mountains, no Colorado
  • Oversaturated, Instagram-filter look
  • Branded uniform shirt (too corporate for the character)
  • Looking at the camera and smiling (he looks at the job)
  • Neon, purple, or anything that reads "tech startup"
Color Grade Direction
  • Shadows: warm — pull toward brown, not blue
  • Highlights: amber/gold — morning or evening light
  • Saturation: slightly pulled back — filmic, not vivid
  • Contrast: medium-high — define the shadows
  • Skin tone: warm, natural — not overexposed
Shot Composition
  • Rule of thirds — character off-center when landscape matters
  • Give negative space on the side The Brinkman is looking
  • Low angle when the sky and mountains are part of the story
  • Close on tools when the job is the subject
  • Wide establishing shot when introducing a new job
04 — Character Design

Illustration Parameters

When The Brinkman appears as an illustrated character (not photography), these parameters apply. They define what makes an image recognizably The Brinkman across any platform, any format, any variation of situation.

Fixed Elements (Never Change)
  • Red plaid flannel — the signature garment
  • Work overalls — rust-brown, worn, real
  • Heavy work boots — dark, serious
  • Beanie or work cap — mountain casual
  • Mustache — the face signature
  • Wrench or hand tool always present
  • Coffee cup — nearly always in frame
Variable Elements (Change by Scene)
  • Tool in hand — varies by the job
  • Background — Colorado mountains, properties, interiors
  • Time of day — golden hour preferred, but flexible
  • Body position — working, assessing, standing after
  • Expression shift — same base (steady/amused), slight variation

Format Modifiers

  • meme format, text space top/bottom

  • Rocky Mountains in background

Character Modifiers

07 — Brand Rules

Do. Don't.

The visual rules are not aesthetic preferences. They are the difference between content that builds trust and content that erodes it.

Always
  • Warm color palette — amber, rust, sage, dust
  • Red plaid flannel on the character, always
  • Colorado in the frame — mountain, sky, or property
  • Real textures — grain, worn material, weathered wood
  • Cinematic framing even on quick content
  • Consistent character across every image
  • Coffee. Almost always.
Never
  • Blue or purple dominant color schemes
  • Stock photo style — posed, sterile, happy
  • Multiple variations of the character look (no alternate outfits)
  • Generic "handyman" iconography — clip art, simple icons
  • Fonts other than the three in the system
  • Emoji in formal brand materials
  • Anything that looks like it could be from a national franchise
